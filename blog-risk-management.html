How to Manage Risk During High Volatility: Position Sizing and Capital Preservation

Elena Rostova

Risk Management Specialist, Stackly

Volatility is a double-edged sword. It is the fuel that creates price movements and profitable opportunities, yet it is also the primary driver of catastrophic trading account drawdowns. When markets shift into high-volatility regimes, traditional parameters must be adjusted to defend equity curves.

In this article, we outline a mathematically rigorous approach to capital preservation during volatile macro environments, detailing position sizing models, stops adjustments, and correlation management.

"Amateur traders focus on how much they can make. Professional traders focus exclusively on how much they can lose. If you control the downside, the upside will take care of itself." — Elena Rostova, Risk Management Specialist

1. The Golden 1% Rule

The foundation of capital preservation is the percentage risk rule. As a baseline, you should never risk more than 1% to 2% of your total account equity on any single trade setup. If you run a $50,000 account, a 1% risk limit means your stop-loss, if hit, will trigger a loss of exactly $500.

Adhering to this rule ensures that a string of losses (which happens to every trader) does not lead to terminal drawdowns. For example, a trader risking 1% per trade can withstand 10 consecutive losses and still preserve approximately 90% of their starting capital. A trader risking 10% per trade would lose over 65% of their capital in the same scenario.

2. Volatility-Adjusted Stops (The ATR Method)

During normal volatility, a 20-pip stop on a currency pair might be sufficient. During periods of macroeconomic news releases, the same pair might swing 50 pips in a minute. If you keep your stop-loss distances fixed, you will be shaken out prematurely.

To avoid this, use the Average True Range (ATR) indicator to measure volatility. Set your stop-loss distance as a multiple of the current ATR (e.g., 2.0 x ATR). When volatility spikes, your stop-loss widens, allowing the trade space to breathe. To maintain the 1% risk rule, you must decrease your trade size proportionally as the stop-loss distance increases.

3. The Correlation Trap: Diversification Illusion

Many traders believe they are diversified because they hold five different trades. However, if those positions are in highly correlated assets—such as EUR/USD, GBP/USD, and AUD/USD, which are all inversely tied to the U.S. Dollar Index (DXY)—they are effectively running a single massive trade.

When volatility strikes, correlations tend to converge toward 1.0. If the USD surges, all three of those positions will likely hit their stop losses simultaneously. Limit your exposure to any single currency group or market sector to protect your account against unexpected systemic moves.

4. Leverage: The Accelerator of Ruin

Leverage allows you to control larger positions with smaller capital outlays. While leverage can boost returns during quiet markets, it operates as an accelerator of ruin in highly volatile regimes.

Consider reducing your effective leverage (the actual size of your positions divided by your account equity) during high-volatility weeks. By trading smaller nominal sizes and relying on wider, volatility-adjusted stop losses, you keep the dollar risk constant while minimizing the impact of slippage and execution spread widening.

Risk Mitigation Checklist

  • Determine stop loss before entry: Never open a trade without knowing exactly where you will exit if you are wrong.
  • Adjust size, not stop: If the market requires a wider stop loss, lower your unit sizing. Do not widen your stop loss without adjusting position size.
  • Avoid news trading: Spreads widen significantly during high-impact news releases, causing slippage. Step aside or clear positions before major macro events.
