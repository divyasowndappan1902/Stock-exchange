5 Technical Indicators Every Trader Needs: MACD, RSI, and Beyond

David Hayes

Senior Market Strategist, Stackly

Navigating the financial markets without technical indicators is like sailing in open water without a compass. While price action remains the ultimate source of truth, indicators compress complex volume, momentum, and trend data into actionable visual representations.

In this guide, we break down five essential technical indicators that every trader—regardless of assets traded—should incorporate into their analysis to build a robust statistical edge.

"Technical indicators do not predict the future. Instead, they help us classify current market conditions and assess the probability of upcoming trends." — David Hayes, Senior Market Strategist

1. MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)

Developed by Gerald Appel, the MACD is a trend-following momentum indicator. It displays the relationship between two moving averages of a security’s price—typically the 12-period and 26-period Exponential Moving Averages (EMAs).

Traders watch for two main signals: Signal Line Crossovers (when the MACD line crosses above or below the 9-period EMA signal line) and Zero-Line Crossovers. Centerline crossovers indicate shifts in momentum, while divergences between MACD and price can warn of potential reversals.

2. RSI (Relative Strength Index)

The RSI, created by J. Welles Wilder, is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and change of price movements. Its scale ranges from 0 to 100.

Traditionally, an RSI value above 70 indicates an overbought condition, implying the asset may be due for a correction. Conversely, an RSI below 30 signals an oversold condition. Strong trends, however, can keep RSI overbought or oversold for extended periods, making it useful to look for RSI failures and centerline crosses to confirm momentum shifts.

3. Moving Averages (SMA & EMA)

Moving averages smooth out price fluctuations to show the underlying trend. The Simple Moving Average (SMA) calculates the average price over a set period, while the Exponential Moving Average (EMA) gives greater weight to recent data, making it more responsive to new information.

Popular periods include the 50-day moving average (intermediate trend support) and the 200-day moving average (long-term market health benchmark). Crossovers, such as the "Golden Cross" (50 SMA crossing above 200 SMA), are widely monitored macro indicators.

4. Bollinger Bands

Bollinger Bands, formulated by John Bollinger, consist of a center band (usually a 20-period SMA) and two outer bands set at two standard deviations above and below the center line. They expand and contract based on market volatility.

When the bands contract (known as a "squeeze"), it signals low volatility, which is often followed by explosive breakouts. When the price consistently tags the upper band, the market is considered overextended; tagging the lower band implies undervaluation.

5. Stochastic Oscillator

The Stochastic Oscillator compares a security's closing price to its price range over a specific time period. It consists of two lines, %K and %D, and oscillates between 0 and 100.

Similar to RSI, readings above 80 indicate overbought conditions, while readings below 20 indicate oversold. Traders often look for crossovers of the %K line above the %D line in oversold regions as a confirmation signal for buying.

Summary Rules for Strategy Integration

  • Avoid Redundancy: Don't combine multiple indicators of the same type (e.g., using both RSI and Stochastics, which are both momentum oscillators). Instead, pair a momentum oscillator with a trend filter (like EMA).
  • Context is King: Indicators perform differently based on market regime. Oscillators excel in range-bound markets, while moving averages are most effective in strong trends.
  • Confirm with Price Action: Never trade an indicator signal blindly. Ensure that key support or resistance levels match the indicator's directional bias.
